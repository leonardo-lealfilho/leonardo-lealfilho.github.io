Case Study 2: Attrition

Attrition is when an employee voluntarily leaves a company. Since the employee is the one who decided to terminate the relationship with the company, such actions can be costly for a company who would then need to replace that talent. This project seeks to find features that could be useful for understanding employee’s decision to leave the company.

The structure shows that there are more numerical features than there are categorical. The summary shows that some of the numerical features could be understood as categorical because they have very low range, such as 1 to 4 or 1 to 5 in some cases. The other detail is that features like EmployeeCount and StandardHours have the same value for all of the datapoints so their use is questionable as far as their ability to detect attrition, The information that they offer is that each data point is representative of a single employee and that the standard working hours for all employees is the same 80 hours.

The good news about this initial data exploration is the satisfaction to know that there are no missing values. Because of that, I do not see a need to make any changes to the data set.

The Monthly Income data shows that the Income of those who have decided to voluntarily leave the company seem to be lower than the “No” Attrition group, same goes for the Age data. In contrast, the Distance from homw boxplot shows that the “Yes” Attrition group seem to live on average farther away from work than those who do not. Now to built a scatterplot the two features.

The plots show that the the monthly income and total working years are features that concentrate the people who chooses to leave in the lower end of both spectrums. Because there are so few data points that represents the “Yes” in the Attrition group, features that concentrate those data points near each other can be very useful when building a classification model.

The classification model I will build will be a K nearest neighbor. In building this model I will be deviding the dataset into train and test, a 70-30 split. To be sure that I will have enough of the “Yes” Attrition category to train and test the model, I will pick 70% of it to the training set and 30% of it to the testing set. This means that the training set will composed of 70% of the entire “No” Attrition as well as 70% of the “Yes” Attrition. Another detail about building the model is that I will be looping through 15,000 different random selections of the training and testing data, and that loop will be nested with another loop that will test how many k neighbors (from 1 to 5) would make for a model that will produce the best specificity. The reason why I want the best specificity is because I want a model that will be able to better detect the “Yes” Attritions.

# Checking the best k's in the knn model

These features are very good at predicting the Attrition status by simply have 1 single k as the nearest neighbor. The models were still able to produce a significantly high specificity score. The reason why the Specificity score is so important is because of the way it is calculated. The specificity in this case is calculated by dividing all the predicted points that were righfully predicted as “Yes” Attrition by all of the “Yes” Attrition present in the testing data set.

With that we can conclude that the monthly income and the distance from home are two factors that can drive talent out of the company. Now I will work on finding categorical features that may seem to increase the likelihood of attrition.

The last insight from the data will be to check what can affect the young talent into deciding to leave the company. For that, I will use data point where the Education level is 4 or 5.

The plots shows that despite being working for the company for approximately the same amount of time, the young people with the highest education levels have lower income opportunity than their older peers. Those could be reasons driving top young talent to the competition.

Conclussion

The data does speak a lot as to what could be driving employees to seek “better opportunities” else where. Out of the folks that has chosen to leave the company, they on average are younger with lower income who live farther from the place of work. Perhaps increasing those employees salary to help with the cost of moving closer or with the cost of transportation could help keep those employees hapily employed in the company. Specially since the young talent may also include professionals with high levels of education and similar company experience as their older counterparts, but who may not be feeling as respected.
